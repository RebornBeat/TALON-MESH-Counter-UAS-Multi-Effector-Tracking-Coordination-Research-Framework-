## 1. Purpose

This document specifies the algorithmic core of TALON-MESH’s decision layer. It details the strategies used to solve the fundamental problem of Counter-UAS coordination: **"Which effector addresses which target, and when?"**

Unlike traditional tracking systems that aim at a single point, TALON-MESH must optimize assignments against a **probability field** of predictive centers generated by PentaTrack, constrained by real-world physics like Line-of-Sight (LOS) and effector slew rates.

This document covers:
1.  **Problem Formulation:** From simple 1-to-1 matching to Multi-Center coverage.
2.  **Algorithmic Stack:** Hungarian, Auction, and Greedy Set Cover.
3.  **Constraint Integration:** How Line-of-Sight and Geometry modify costs.
4.  **Saturation Policies:** Behavior when targets exceed effector capacity.

## 2. Problem Formulation

### 2.1. The Standard Assignment Problem
In the baseline case, we have $M$ effectors and $N$ targets.
*   **Objective:** Minimize the total cost (e.g., sum of distances) or maximize total utility.
*   **Constraint:** Each effector addresses at most one target; each target is addressed by at most one effector.

This is a classic bipartite matching problem solvable via the **Hungarian Algorithm**.

### 2.2. The Multi-Center Assignment Problem
TALON-MESH does not treat a target as a single point. A target tracked by PentaTrack is represented by a set of $K$ predictive centers $C = \{c_1, c_2, ..., c_k\}$, each with a probability weight $p_i$.

*   **Objective:** Maximize the total probability mass "covered" by the array.
*   **Formulation:**
    Let $x_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$ be a binary variable indicating if effector $i$ is assigned to center $j$.
    $$ \text{Maximize: } Z = \sum_{i=1}^{M} \sum_{j=1}^{K} p_j \cdot x_{ij} $$
    Subject to:
    $$ \sum_{j=1}^{K} x_{ij} \leq 1 \quad \forall i \quad (\text{Each effector addresses one center}) $$

This transforms the problem from a 2D matrix ($Effectors \times Targets$) to a **3D tensor** ($Effectors \times Targets \times Centers$).

## 3. The Algorithmic Stack

Different scenarios require different algorithmic approaches. TALON-MESH implements a modular stack allowing researchers to switch between strategies.

### 3.1. Baseline: The Hungarian Algorithm
**File:** `software/decision/algorithms/hungarian.py`
**Use Case:** Simple 1-to-1 matching when targets are treated as single points (geometric centroids) for baseline comparison.

*   **Mechanism:** Solves the assignment problem in $O(n^3)$ time.
*   **Pros:** Guarantees the optimal solution for single-point targets.
*   **Cons:** Fails to account for the probability distribution of the target's future position. Treats a "certain" hit and a "probable" hit identically if distances are equal.

### 3.2. Distributed: The Auction Algorithm
**File:** `software/decision/algorithms/auction.py`
**Use Case:** Decentralized systems where effectors operate with local information.

*   **Mechanism:**
    1.  Effectors "bid" on targets based on the utility of engaging them (inverse of cost).
    2.  Targets are assigned to the highest bidder.
    3.  Prices adjust until equilibrium is reached.
*   **Pros:**
    *   Parallelizable: Effectors can compute bids asynchronously.
    *   Robust to communication latency in distributed sensor networks.
*   **Cons:** Requires iteration to converge; generally slower than Hungarian for small, centralized problems.

### 3.3. Advanced: Greedy Set Cover with Budget
**File:** `software/decision/algorithms/greedy_cover.py`
**Use Case:** The primary algorithm for **Multi-Center Engagement**. This is the default strategy for TALON-MESH when $N$ (array elements) > 1.

*   **Problem:** We have $N$ "slots" (array elements) and want to cover the "universe" of target probability mass.
*   **Mechanism:**
    1.  **Input:** The PentaTrack center field $C$ and a budget $N$.
    2.  **Step 1:** Select the center $c_{best}$ with the highest effective weight $w_{eff}$.
        $$ w_{eff}(c) = p_{nominal}(c) \cdot \text{DriftBonus}(c) $$
    3.  **Step 2:** Assign an effector element to $c_{best}$. Decrement budget $N$.
    4.  **Step 3:** Update the "marginal gain" of remaining centers. (If two centers are close, covering one reduces the value of the other).
    5.  **Repeat** until budget $N = 0$.

*   **Theoretical Guarantee:**
    The objective function for coverage is **Submodular**.
    The Greedy algorithm achieves an approximation ratio of $(1 - 1/e) \approx 0.63$ of the optimal solution. For NP-hard set cover problems, this is the best possible polynomial-time approximation for general cases.

## 4. Constraint Integration

Algorithms do not operate in a vacuum. The cost functions used by Hungarian, Auction, or Greedy methods must integrate physical constraints.

### 4.1. Line-of-Sight (LOS) as a Cost Modifier
(Ref: `docs/theory/line_of_sight_geometry.md`)

The naive distance cost $D_{euclidean}$ is replaced by an **Effective Cost**:

$$ Cost_{eff}(e, t) = \frac{D_{euclidean}}{LOS_{quality}(e, t)} $$

*   If $LOS_{quality} \to 1.0$ (Clear path): Cost equals distance.
*   If $LOS_{quality} \to 0.0$ (Obstructed): Cost $\to \infty$.

**Implementation:**
In the Greedy algorithm, centers with $LOS_{quality} < \text{Threshold}$ are simply filtered out of the candidate set before selection.

### 4.2. Slew Time and Geometry
Effector elements cannot teleport.
*   **Constraint:** An effector cannot address a center if the required slew time exceeds the prediction horizon (time until the target reaches that center).
*   **Logic:**
    $$ \text{Feasible}(e, c) = \begin{cases} \text{True} & \text{if } t_{slew}(e \to c) < t_{horizon} \\ \text{False} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$
    Where $t_{slew}$ depends on the effector's angular velocity capabilities.

### 4.3. Drift-Aware Weighting
PentaTrack's drift analysis identifies the "Drift Leader"—the center that has historically been the most accurate predictor.
*   **Modification:**
    $$ w_{eff}(c) = p_{nominal}(c) \times (1 + \alpha \cdot \mathbb{I}_{leader}(c)) $$
    Where $\alpha$ is a policy-configurable bonus (e.g., 0.2 for a 20% boost) and $\mathbb{I}_{leader}$ is an indicator function. This biases the assignment toward continuity with past success.

## 5. Multi-Target Coordination Under Saturation

What happens when Targets ($T$) > Effectors ($E$)?

### 5.1. Priority Weighting
Each target $t$ has a priority score $Pr(t)$ derived from:
*   **Velocity:** Fast incoming targets are higher priority.
*   **Classification:** "Armed UAS" > " Surveillance UAS" > "Bird".
*   **Distance:** Closer targets are higher priority.

### 5.2. Capacity Allocation
When $T > E$, the system runs a **Global Allocation Step** before detailed assignment:
1.  Rank targets by $Pr(t)$.
2.  Allocate effector capacity $N_t$ to each target such that $\sum N_t = E$.
    *   *Simple Policy:* Top $E$ targets get 1 slot each; others get 0.
    *   *Weighted Policy:* High-priority targets get multiple slots (hedging), low-priority get 0.

### 5.3. Starvation Prevention
To prevent low-priority targets from being ignored indefinitely, an **"Age" factor** is added to priority:
$$ Pr_{eff}(t) = Pr_{base}(t) + \beta \cdot \text{TimeSinceLastEngaged}(t) $$
This ensures that eventually, even low-priority targets receive attention if they remain in the volume.

## 6. Dynamic Re-Assignment

The assignment is not static. It updates every frame ($\Delta t \approx 10-50ms$).

### 6.1. Hysteresis
To prevent "thrashing" (rapidly switching effectors between two equally good targets), a **Hysteresis Penalty** is applied:
$$ Cost_{new} = Cost_{eff} + \gamma \cdot \mathbb{I}_{change} $$
Where $\gamma$ is a penalty for changing an existing assignment. Effectors stay on their current target unless the benefit of switching significantly outweighs the cost of moving.

### 6.2. Handoff Logic
As a target moves across the boundary of Zone A to Zone B:
1.  Zone A's effector tracks until LOS quality drops.
2.  Zone B's effector is pre-spun/aligned.
3.  Assignment algorithm seamlessly switches the "active" effector.
(See `docs/theory/multi_center_engagement.md` for the geometric derivation).

## 7. Implementation Details

### 7.1. Complexity
*   **Hungarian:** $O(n^3)$ — Feasible for $n < 50$.
*   **Greedy Set Cover:** $O(N \cdot |C|)$ — Extremely fast. Even with $N=100$ elements and $|C|=27$ centers (3D PentaTrack), this is negligible compute time, suitable for embedded deployment in simulation.

### 7.2. Data Structures
*   **Cost Matrix:** `numpy.ndarray` of shape $(M, N)$ for baseline.
*   **Assignment Object:**
    ```python
    class Assignment:
        effector_id: str
        target_id: str
        center_index: int        # Which predictive center is addressed
        confidence: float        # Probability covered
        los_quality: float       # Geometric feasibility
    ```

## 8. Civilian Transfer

These algorithms are mathematically identical to problems in logistics and robotics:

### 8.1. Multi-Cobot Coordination
*   **Problem:** $M$ robots, $N$ tasks.
*   **Transfer:** "Targets" become "Tasks." "Priority" becomes "Task Urgency." The Hungarian/Auction algorithms are standard in warehouse robotics (e.g., Amazon warehouse robots).

### 8.2. Multi-Camera Sports Tracking
*   **Problem:** $M$ cameras, $N$ players.
*   **Transfer:** "Effectors" become "PTZ Cameras." "LOS" becomes "View Obstruction by other players."
*   **Use Case:** Ensuring that all players are covered by at least one camera. The Greedy Set Cover algorithm optimizes for "Total Player Visibility."

### 8.3. Ride-Sharing / Dispatch
*   **Problem:** $M$ drivers, $N$ riders.
*   **Transfer:** Minimizing wait time (Cost) vs Maximizing rider satisfaction (Priority).
